# Money & Policies

How Orbit PowerWash gets paid, handles problems, and keeps its books. Plain rules a solo owner can actually follow. Business facts (phone, email) are in `company-onepager.md`; the tool setup is in `systems-stack.md`.

---

## Payment Policy

**Payment is due on completion** — when the job's done and the customer has seen the result, they pay. Same day.

**Accepted methods, in order of preference:**
1. **Zelle** — no fees, lands in your bank. Ask for this first.
2. **Cash** — fine, always hand over a receipt.
3. **Venmo** — fee-free for standard personal transfers; watch the payment clear before you leave.
4. **Card** — last resort only, through a free-tier processor. It takes a percentage off the top, so don't offer it by default.

**No deposits for standard residential jobs in week one.** You're a brand-new business asking strangers to trust you — the fastest way to lose a booking is to demand money up front before they've seen your work. Reduce friction: quote it, book it, do it, get paid on completion. Building a client base and a wall of reviews matters more this week than protecting against a rare no-pay.

**When a deposit *is* warranted** (use judgment, not every job):
- The job needs materials or a rental you'd have to buy specifically for it.
- It's a large multi-surface job well above a normal residential quote.
- The customer asks to book far out and you're holding a prime slot.
- A prior red flag (a bounced payment, a serial rescheduler).

If you take a deposit, keep it small (e.g., ~25%), say so plainly in the text thread, and subtract it from the final total on the invoice.

## Refund / Redo Policy

If a customer isn't happy, you fix it — you don't argue.

1. **Redo first.** Offer to come back and re-clean the spot that fell short, at no charge. Most complaints are a missed area or a fresh set of eyes, and a redo turns an unhappy customer into a review.
2. **Refund is the last resort** — only when a redo genuinely won't solve it or the customer refuses one.
3. **The owner decides, same day.** You're solo — there's no committee. Don't leave a complaint sitting; a fast, fair answer protects your reputation more than the money you'd save by stalling.

Keep it in the text thread so the resolution is on record.

## Cancellation & Weather Policy

**Customer cancels:** No fee. Be gracious and ask to rebook — a rescheduled job is still a job. ("No problem at all — want me to grab you a spot later this week?")

**Rain / bad weather:** You reschedule **proactively the night before**. Don't make the customer chase you, and don't show up to wash in a downpour. Check the forecast every evening as part of the admin routine and get ahead of it.

**Weather reschedule text (use verbatim, fill the brackets):**

```
Hi [Name], it's [Your name] with Orbit PowerWash. Tomorrow's
forecast turned to rain, so I'd rather move your [service]
to a dry day and do it right. I've got [day] or [day] open —
which works better for you? Thanks for your patience!
```

## Record-Keeping

**Track every dollar in and out in one sheet.** This is Tab 2 of your Google Sheet (see `systems-stack.md`). Don't overthink it — one row per transaction.

**Money sheet columns:**

| Column | Example |
|---|---|
| Date | 2026-07-21 |
| In / Out | In |
| Customer / Vendor | Rivera |
| Description | Driveway Washing |
| Amount | 175.00 |
| Method | Zelle |
| Invoice # | 0001 |
| Receipt saved? | Y / N |

- **Every job you get paid for = one "In" row.** Every business expense = one "Out" row.
- **Save receipts for fuel and chemicals** (and any equipment, rentals, or supplies). Photograph each one into a "Receipts" folder in Drive the day you spend the money — this is how you prove expenses later. Mark "Receipt saved? Y" once it's filed.
- At the end of the week, In minus Out shows what you actually cleared. That number feeds `week-one-review.md`.

### [NOT LEGAL OR TAX ADVICE]

The items below are **things to verify with a qualified NJ professional** (an accountant, a bookkeeper, an insurance agent, and/or an attorney). This is a checklist of questions to ask — **not** answers, and **not** a statement that any of them do or don't apply to you. Don't guess on these; a short paid consult early is cheaper than a wrong assumption.

- **Business registration:** how to properly register the business in New Jersey and whether you need a specific structure or filing before operating.
- **Insurance:** what liability (and any other) coverage a pressure-washing business should carry, and getting it in place before you're on someone else's property. Your website mentions insurance verification — make sure the reality matches the claim.
- **Sales tax on services in NJ:** whether your services are taxable in New Jersey, and if so how to register to collect and remit it. Rules on taxability of services vary — confirm before you price and invoice, so you're not surprised.
- **Income taxes & records:** how to report business income, what records to keep, and what expenses are deductible — the money sheet above is your starting point for that conversation.
- **Local requirements:** whether Plainsboro, West Windsor, or the county require any local business license, permit, or water-runoff/wastewater rules for pressure washing.

Verify each with a professional before relying on it.
